Dear Editor,

We submit for your consideration our manuscript entitled "Heterogeneous effects of iron supplementation in Brazilian infants: a quasi-experimental causal machine learning study with clinical tool deployment" for publication in *The Lancet Digital Health*.

Iron supplementation is recommended for over 100 million infants worldwide through universal programmes, yet the Pemba trial demonstrated that this intervention can increase hospitalisations and deaths. Whether similar harms occur outside malaria-endemic African settings, and which individual infants are most likely to benefit or be harmed, has remained unknown. Our study addresses this gap using a combination of traditional epidemiology and causal machine learning applied to a nationally representative Brazilian survey (ENANI-2019, n=3127). We believe *The Lancet Digital Health* is the ideal venue because this study demonstrates how causal machine learning can be translated from methodological innovation to a deployed clinical tool -- bridging the gap between computational advances and health practice that the journal's mission addresses.

We believe this manuscript merits consideration for four reasons:

**First, our findings directly challenge current Brazilian guidelines with immediate policy relevance.** The Brazilian Society of Paediatrics (SBP) updated its recommendations in 2024 to advance iron prophylaxis to 90 days of life for all infants, irrespective of breastfeeding status. We demonstrate that breastfeeding is the primary modifier of iron's effects -- independently confirmed by both epidemiological interaction analysis (p=0.003) and data-driven SHAP feature importance -- and that exclusively breastfed infants aged 6--11 months show minimal haemoglobin benefit alongside increased infectious morbidity. These findings have implications for universal supplementation programmes in low- and middle-income countries worldwide.

**Second, this is the first application of Causal Forest (double machine learning) to paediatric nutritional epidemiology in a low- or middle-income country.** We exploit the quasi-experimental variation generated by differential adherence to Brazil's universal iron supplementation programme (PNSF) -- where the policy recommends supplementation for all infants regardless of iron status, making exposure variation a function of healthcare access rather than clinical indication. A formal causal identification strategy including a directed acyclic graph, quantitative bias analysis, propensity score weighting, E-values, and a negative control outcome supports causal interpretation despite the cross-sectional design.

**Third, we deploy a freely available, open-source clinical decision tool** that integrates five outcome dimensions (haemoglobin, ferritin, growth, infectious morbidity, and diarrhoea) into an individualised benefit--harm assessment, accessible at https://marcelosilva2604.github.io/IronBrazil/. The tool runs entirely client-side with gradient boosting surrogate models achieving R^2^ 0.96--0.99 against the original Causal Forest predictions. We present this as a research prototype for hypothesis generation, not a validated clinical instrument, in accordance with the DECIDE-AI framework.

**Fourth, the equity analysis reveals that iron supplementation is an equity-positive intervention**, with an 11-fold gradient in ponderal growth benefit across wealth quintiles (poorest: CATE +0.056 z-score; wealthiest: +0.005, non-significant) and no racial disparities. This finding argues for preserving supplementation for socioeconomically disadvantaged populations while potentially targeting the approach for exclusively breastfed infants -- a nuance absent from current one-size-fits-all guidelines.

A novel mechanistic finding strengthens the biological narrative: exclusively breastfed infants show the highest ferritin CATEs (+4.1 ng/mL) despite the lowest haemoglobin CATEs (+0.023 g/dL), a dissociation consistent with lactoferrin-mediated iron sequestration limiting systemic bioavailability while still replenishing hepatic stores. This transforms the lactoferrin hypothesis from a narrative explanation into a testable mechanism with empirical support.

We propose concrete next steps for the Brazilian context, including a cluster-randomised trial within the PNSF, a registry-based cohort linking SISVAN supplementation records to longitudinal morbidity outcomes, and a prospective pilot integrating the clinical decision tool into routine paediatric consultations.

We confirm that this manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. All authors have approved the manuscript and declare no competing interests. This study received no external funding.
